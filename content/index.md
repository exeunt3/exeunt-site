### Regenerative Design in the Many Worlds
<details>
  <summary><strong>LLM Summary</strong></summary>
  <p>This essay introduces the **Many Worlds Interpretation (MWI)** of quantum mechanics as a philosophical substrate for a post-extractive, pluralistic ethics. It proposes that **observer paths**, far from isolated, participate in a **rulial multiplicity**—a term borrowed from Stephen Wolfram—to describe the swarming, recombining space of multiversal becoming. Against the dominant ideology of scarcity and centralization, the essay frames **creative agency** as embedded in and responsive to this multiplicity. From this emerges the concept of **regenerative design**, which reframes _technē_ and _poiesis_ as collaborative processes of material care and ontological innovation. The essay introduces **Distributed Autonomous Technologies (DATs)** as infrastructural tools that encode these ethics: plural, bioregional, and anti-homogenizing. DATs function as worldbuilding mechanisms, combining autonomy and interdependence in recursive, open-ended cycles of innovation. These tools are situated within both local commons and global knowledge networks, forming the material and epistemological substrate for what the essay terms a **federation of worlds**—distributed, co-creative systems of production and meaning-making.</p>

### On the Ontological Stakes of the Moloch Meme
<details>
  <summary><strong>LLM Summary</strong></summary>
  <p>This essay interrogates the popular "Moloch meme"—Scott Alexander’s game-theoretic account of coordination failure—and uncovers its hidden ontological assumptions. While coordination remains vital, the essay contends that **coordination maximalism** alone is insufficient for addressing problems rooted in **ontological violence**, such as white supremacy and the logic of exclusion baked into Western subjectivity. 
  Drawing on Black critical theory (Moten, Sexton, Hartman) and posthuman systems thinkers (Goertzel, Weinbaum, Veitas), the essay proposes a turn from **reward-maximizing rationality** to a model of **Open-Ended Intelligence**: a distributed, reflexive, and ontologically open mode of sense-making and individuation. This mode dissolves the myth of the sovereign agent (the “I AM” that opposes Moloch) and instead centers a **field of individuation** in which agency is emergent, collective, and dynamic.
  It calls for the encoding of **ontological pluralism** into technical and social systems, suggesting that design must go beyond incentive engineering toward cultivating **mundane daily practices of interbeing**. The smart contract and the DAO become sites not just of coordination, but of **ontological experimentation**—stages for **embodied pluralism** rather than hollow sovereignty.</p>
</details>

### Fourth of July in Network Space
<details>
  <summary><strong>LLM Summary</strong></summary>
  <p>This essay reexamines **freedom** not as ownership, autonomy, or legal right, but as a **relational force**—the capacity to be unavailable for capture, to resist being turned into an object within someone else's logic of control. It traces this concept through the layered history of Lichfield, England—a site marked by enclosure, rebellion, prophecy, and myth—and frames it as a symbolic node for the longer arc of how **sovereignty and legitimacy are constructed**.
  Rather than grounding freedom in possession or violence (the “I AM” of divine right), the essay proposes a freedom grounded in **networked presence**, where legitimacy is not declared but **emerges through encounters, synchronicities, and social architectures**. Enclosure, as the founding act of scarcity and control, is opposed by a **distributed field of singularities**—moments, agents, and acts that resist being enclosed.
  Crypto protocols and DAOs are proposed as modern **technologies of open legitimacy**, capable of encoding this more fertile, plural, and emergent version of freedom. Here, **freedom becomes a pattern of coordination without domination**, a process of negotiated sovereignty built through trust and resonance rather than control.</p>

Blockchain, in this context, joins a lineage of urban vernaculars—graffiti, rhythms, hybrid codes—as a **public protocol for distributed meaning-making and material agency**.</p>

It rejects the narrow vision of “technology” as digital novelty or scalable solutionism, arguing instead for a broader ethic of **adequate technology**—tools and systems that fit their terrain, support autonomy, and invite mutual transformation.

It encourages Ethereum communities to engage not just metaphorically but materially—with the rhythms, needs, and logics of cities and bioregions.

The goal is not to impose blockchain logic on the world, but to recognize and contribute to a shared **underground of open protocols**—social, technical, artistic—already active and evolving. Ethereum Localism becomes a commitment to that entangled project: one rooted in immediacy, craft, anti-authoritarian pluralism, and the mystery of what can be built together.</p>

### We Are the Neotechnics!
<details>
  <summary><strong>LLM Summary</strong></summary>
  <p>This essay reframes Vitalik Buterin’s philosophy of **defensive technological acceleration (d/acc)** through the historical lens of **Lewis Mumford’s “neotechnic revolution”**—a period of distributed, electricity-enabled innovation that offered a path away from centralized, extractive power systems. By aligning Buterin’s optimism with Mumford’s vision, the essay argues for a synthesis between web3 infrastructure and **decentralized material production**.
  Drawing on Kevin Carson’s work, it outlines three historical stages of technological development: the **eotechnic era** of localized, renewable power; the **paleotechnic era** of steam-driven centralization; and the **neotechnic era**, which promised a fusion of urban and rural autonomy through distributed electrical tools and shared information flows. While the technical conditions for neotechnic flourishing now exist—especially in places like Portland’s maker underground—the political and economic systems haven’t caught up.
  The essay proposes that **web3 can serve as the missing coordination layer**: linking isolated local producers into collaborative ecosystems that are economically resilient and structurally autonomous. Ethereum protocols, commons-based finance, and low-overhead governance models could finally help **scale neotechnic ideals** across regions, bridging the gap from personal independence to shared infrastructure.</p>
</details>

At the same time, urban open protocols offer lessons in **resilience, stealth, and cultural sustainability**—lessons that could ground web3’s abstract architectures in lived, embodied forms of shared life. Together, these systems point toward a new kind of decentralized social fabric—**self-constituted, nonrivalrous, and prefigurative**.</p>

Their improvisational, pluralistic, and resilient nature makes them laboratories of social production that crypto could learn from—if it pays attention.

  The essay closes by asking: how can we **overground** these undergrounds—formalize their spirit without standardizing it? How do we ensure that the protocols we build serve high-agency, pluralistic culture rather than institutional replication? What does it mean to take freedom seriously, not just as a claim, but as a practice of co-constituted vibe and continuous consent?</p>

### Undercapital
(with Open Protocol Research Group)
<details>
  <summary><strong>LLM Summary</strong></summary>
  <p>This essay introduces **undercapital**, a design-oriented way of thinking about value—one that moves beyond financial metrics to consider the real but often invisible conditions that make creativity, coordination, and cultural life possible. It asks: _What kinds of capital support freedom, improvisation, and new forms of shared meaning?_ And how can we design for that?
  Undercapital emerges from observing **underground spaces**—DIY communities, artistic scenes, autonomous labs, and other off-grid ecosystems—where value is generated not by institutions, but through **open protocols**, flexible and often informal systems of collaboration and trust. These environments aren’t unstructured—they’re deeply structured, just in a way that resists centralization, standardization, or commercial capture.
  The essay argues that these spaces don’t scale by getting bigger or more uniform. Instead, they replicate through difference—forking, mutating, adapting locally. What they need isn’t more oversight, but better tools for **resource flow, coordination, and signaling**—tools that preserve autonomy, consent, and pluralism. This is where the lens of undercapital becomes useful: not just as critique, but as a generative field for new economic and social design.
  Crucially, undercapital isn’t anti-money—it’s a proposal to **rewire how capital and coordination work together**, in ways that recognize the real, often immeasurable richness of social fields, environments, and potential. Drawing from philosophy, underground culture, and protocol engineering, the essay builds toward a vision of decentralized systems that can host the complexity and aliveness of human (and more-than-human) activity—without freezing it into fixed categories.
  If institutions tend to abstract and enclose, undercapital looks for ways to **formalize without standardizing**—to build systems that hold open the space for possibility. That space, the essay suggests, is where our future lives.</p>
</details>

Where undergrounds have deep cultural knowledge but often struggle to scale without compromising their values, Ethereum provides a programmable infrastructure that could allow these forms to grow without losing their soul. The project of “formalization without standardization”—coherence without centralization—becomes the bridge between the protocol-rich life of the underground and the tools of the distributed ledger.
  The stakes are both technical and cultural: can Ethereum help cultural protocols escape their local limits and survive in a wider world, without sacrificing their autonomy or vitality? And in doing so, can Ethereum itself learn from these scenes how to stay weird, participatory, and alive?</p>

### Capitalism, Communism, and the Extitutional Stakes of Our Politics
<details>
  <summary><strong>LLM Summary</strong></summary>
  <p>This piece frames Ethereum as part of a broader cultural shift toward "extitutional space"—a domain of social and economic experimentation that operates outside traditional institutional structures. These spaces are marked by practical adaptations rather than ideological commitments, and are shaped by exclusion from centralized channels of reproduction and authority.
  The author identifies three core dynamics common in extitutional space:
  - **Horizontal scaling**, where protocols and practices adapt contextually rather than replicate uniformly.
  - **Field ontology**, where value and attention are determined relationally, based on real-world conditions, rather than imposed categorical hierarchies.
  - **Open protocolization**, a tendency for practices to evolve as reproducible, adaptable, and empirically validated—rather than hidden, fixed, or ideologically enforced.
  In this framework, both capitalism and communism are understood as institutional ideologies that, in practice, often depend on coercion and enclosure. By contrast, their extitutional counterparts—markets and mutual aid—are seen as decentralized, opt-in modes of interaction that can flourish in open, pluralistic systems.
  Responding to recent discourse in the Ethereum community around perceived ideological leanings, the author argues that mutual aid and resource sharing should not be confused with authoritarian political agendas. Ethereum, in this view, provides a substrate for experimentation and free association—one that accommodates a wide range of behaviors without enforcing ideological conformity or limiting access.
  The article suggests that **mutualism**—understood as an operational rather than ideological framework—offers a promising alignment with Ethereum’s core affordances: permissionless innovation, noncoercive coordination, and parallel social systems. Mutualism, here, is positioned not as a moral stance but as a practical orientation: one that supports autonomy, interoperability, and pluralism without seeking centralized enforcement or cultural gatekeeping.
  Ultimately, the piece calls for Ethereum contributors to focus not on traditional political binaries but on the **real-world mechanics of coercion, access, and system design**—especially where distributed technologies can offer structural alternatives to legacy forms of control. Ethereum’s strength, the author argues, lies in its capacity to support open experimentation across ideological boundaries, and to sustain a culture of practical imagination in post-institutional terrain.</p>

Drawing from thinkers like Brian Massumi, Gilles Deleuze, and Austin Wade Smith, the piece reframes the virtual not as a digital abstraction, but as a dynamic, nondual dimension of reality that shapes affect, imagination, and social coordination.
  Where institutions tend to conceal or enclose these forces—often cloaking them in formality, sentimentality, or mystification—undergrounds assert their presence directly. Through open protocols and experimental forms of organization, they engage the virtual as a practical resource, constructing economies and social systems around states of intensity, atmosphere, and potentiality. This makes undercapital not a metaphor, but a design problem: how do we build for shared access to experiences that resist representation, yet move bodies, shape worlds, and rewire perception?
  In this view, undercapital is the submerged architecture beneath more familiar forms of value. It expands Gregory Landua’s framework of the Eight Forms of Capital by proposing a ninth: _virtual capital_, the felt sense of possibility or becoming that accompanies encounters with meaning, relationality, or intensity for their own sake. This virtual field animates scenes, shapes cultural protocols, and exerts logistical influence even as it remains hard to quantify.
  The essay argues that real social power often flows through these nondual terrains—zones of heightened receptivity, mutual presence, and shared affect—making their democratization and designability key stakes in any future-oriented politics. Rather than leaving them to be captured by institutions, elite spectacle, or ideology, undergrounds circulate protocols that keep access to these states open: how-to guides, consent tools, rituals of immersion, and practices of situated participation. This is what undercapital seeks to formalize—not by standardizing it, but by recognizing it as a vital and usable layer of experience.
  The piece closes by drawing from Austin Wade Smith’s concept of **undualing**: a process of re-opening suppressed or collapsed regimes of experience, making room for new worlding practices that can include a broader cast of intelligences, agencies, and ecological relationships. In this frame, undercapital is both a design vector and an ethical orientation—an effort to re-engage with the full range of material and relational realities that are often excluded by institutional regimes, and to do so on a basis of reciprocity, pluralism, and mutual co-creation.

### Anoma as a Framework for Undercapital Exploration
(with Pam Pascual)

This continuation of the _Undercapital_ project takes up the challenge of translating qualitative, relational, and process-driven dynamics into protocol design. Where the first essay established undercapital as a name for the recessive forces — affective, experiential, and intensive — that shape real-world social coordination but often evade institutional capture, Part Two looks to how these dynamics might be directly hosted within digital infrastructure. The Anoma protocol is explored as a case study in how decentralized systems might move beyond discrete transactions or efficiency metrics to instead facilitate modes of coordination grounded in presence, trust, rhythm, and soft intention. The goal is not to symbolically represent nondual experiences, but to build systems that can hold and respond to them in operational ways.

Drawing on concepts like context-bound signaling, conditional intent expression, and compositional convergence, the essay outlines how a network could be structured to support temporary and emergent social formations — not only users and exchanges, but shared experiences that matter for their own sake. Protocols are framed less as logistical engines and more as scaffolding for situated interactions: rituals, ephemeral groups, affective collectives, or context-aware governance. By rethinking matching, logging, and execution in terms of resonance, memory, and collective sense-making, this work opens a pathway for building systems that align more closely with how people already coordinate meaningfully in unsupervised and experimental settings.
